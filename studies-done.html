Initial research

​In order to fully understand the issue of plastic pollution in Hawaii, it is important to understand the different types of pollution found on beaches. The variety of pollution found on beaches is often traced back to consumers who are prone to littering. A study performed at Armação dos Búzios, a popular tourist resort in Rio de Janeiro (Oigman-Pszczol and Creed, 2007), analyzes the composition and abundance of beach litter.

In a 4-meter wide section of beach that runs parallel to the coastline, almost 16,000 items of litter were collected and categorized into seven groups. The most abundant type of litter was in the form of paper, most often cigarette butts. The most abundant submerged marine debris was plastic in the form of fragments, sheets, bags, and containers. Interestingly, the results from Armação are in accordance with rest of the world, as paper and plastic make up 88% of litter collected in marine environments (Ryan, Moore, Van Franeker, and Moloney, 2009).

Common types include plastic cups, candy wrappers, and bottles. However, most often plastic is found in the form of tiny, unidentifiable colored shards, often referred to as microplastic. Unfortunately, along their journey from consumer to beach, large chunks of plastic have been broken into pieces small enough to be ingested by sea animals (Barnes, Galgani, Thompson, and Barlaz, 2009).

By analyzing and researching the types of pollution found on beaches, it is clear that the litter we produce further inland is analogous to the trash that makes it to our oceans (Earll, Williams, Simmons, and Tudor, 2000). Something needs to be done.
